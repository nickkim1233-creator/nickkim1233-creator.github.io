Part 01

Where to buy
& trade crypto

There are three main places. Think of them like different types of financial institutions — each with real trade-offs.

🏦
Type 01
Centralized Exchange

A company holds your crypto, like a bank holds your dollars. You sign up with email and password. They handle security for you.

Coinbase Kraken Binance Gemini
Easiest start — just sign up and buy
Account recovery if locked out
Bank transfer and card deposits
You don't truly own the coins (the company does)
Can freeze accounts; subject to regulations
⚙️
Type 02
Decentralized Exchange

Trade directly from your own wallet. No company, no signup. Smart contracts run automatically — no humans in the middle.

Uniswap Jupiter Raydium Curve
You hold your coins at all times
Thousands of tokens, including brand new ones
No customer support — mistakes are permanent
Requires a self-custody wallet first
👻
Type 03
Self-Custody Wallet

An app that gives you total ownership. You receive a "seed phrase" (12–24 words) — the master key. Lose it and your funds are gone. No exceptions.

Phantom MetaMask Ledger Trust Wallet
Complete ownership — only you control it
Connects to any DEX
Lost seed phrase = permanent loss, no recovery
No customer support whatsoever
→
Where to start: Buy on a centralized exchange like Coinbase. Once comfortable, move some funds to a wallet like Phantom to explore DEXs and full self-custody.
Part 02

Moving crypto
without losing it

This is where most mistakes happen. Read the first accordion before anything else — it explains the concept that underlies every scenario.

🧠
The one concept you must understand first
Read before any transfer
▾

Think of a wallet address like a phone number, and the blockchain like a phone network. The same coin can travel on different networks — and each network requires a different address format.

⚠
Sending a coin on the wrong network is the #1 way people permanently lose funds. Your coin reaches a valid-looking address — but on the wrong network, so it's unreachable forever.

The rule: Always match the sending network with the correct address type from that same network. The scenarios below show you exactly how.

Scenario 1 — Bitcoin from Coinbase to Phantom
Simple case · Bitcoin network
▾
→
Phantom supports Bitcoin natively. For real BTC on the Bitcoin network, you simply use the Bitcoin address that Phantom gives you.
1
Open Phantom → switch to Bitcoin network
Tap the network selector at the top of Phantom and choose Bitcoin. Your displayed address will be a Bitcoin address.
2
Copy your Bitcoin address
It starts with bc1q, 1, or 3. This is your real Bitcoin address.
3
On Coinbase, go to Send → Bitcoin (BTC)
Paste your Phantom Bitcoin address. When choosing a network, select Bitcoin — not BEP20, ERC20, or Solana.
4
Confirm and wait 10–60 minutes
Bitcoin requires network confirmations. It takes longer than other chains — this is completely normal.
Correct ✓ bc1qar0srrr7xfkvy5l643lydnw9re59gtzzwf5mdq
✓
The simplest case. One coin, one network, one address type. Native BTC on the Bitcoin network.
🌀
Scenario 2 — BTC from Coinbase to Phantom via Solana
Tricky · Wrapped tokens
▾
⚠
Sending BTC "via Solana" means you're not sending real Bitcoin. You're sending a wrapped version called cbBTC — a Solana token tracking Bitcoin's value. You need a Solana address, not a Bitcoin address.

Why do this? Speed. Bitcoin transfers take 10–60 min and cost $2–20 in fees. The same value on Solana takes under 1 second and costs $0.001.

1
Open Phantom → switch to Solana
Make sure you're on Solana. Your address is a long string of mixed letters and numbers — no "bc1" prefix.
2
Copy your Solana address
Looks like: 7XkrMYT5gvp1B9YLpFWpRxDtCv… — 44 characters, no prefix pattern.
3
On Coinbase, select cbBTC (not regular BTC)
Look for cbBTC — Coinbase's wrapped Bitcoin on Solana. This is different from the standard Bitcoin (BTC).
4
Select Solana as the network → paste → send
Confirm the network says Solana. The address must be your 44-character Solana address — not your bc1q Bitcoin address.

Address format comparison

Bitcoin bc1qar0srrr7xfkvy5l643lydnw9re59gtzzwf5mdq
Solana 7XkrMYT5gvp1B9YLpFWpRxDtCvPqY3tPdZbKrjVfQP
⚠ Wrong Bitcoin address sent on Solana network = lost forever
!
Never paste a Bitcoin address when the network is set to Solana. The exchange may allow it — but the funds go somewhere unreachable and cannot be recovered.
💎
Scenario 3 — ETH from Coinbase to MetaMask
Ethereum and its many side-networks
▾

MetaMask uses the same address format across multiple networks — Ethereum, Polygon, Arbitrum, Base, and more. But the network you send on determines where your funds appear.

→
Your MetaMask address (starts with 0x) is identical on all EVM chains. Sending ETH via Polygon means it arrives on Polygon — not Ethereum mainnet. They're separate balances.
1
Copy your MetaMask address (starts with 0x…)
This same address works on Ethereum, Base, Arbitrum, Polygon, and more.
2
Choose your network on Coinbase
Ethereum mainnet is slower and costs more. Base, Arbitrum, or Polygon are faster and far cheaper. Decide before sending.
3
In MetaMask, switch to the matching network
Click the network name at the top and select the same network you sent on. Your ETH will appear there.
✓
If you accidentally sent ETH to MetaMask on the wrong EVM network, you can recover it — just add that network to MetaMask and switch to it. Your funds are there, just not visible yet.
🪙

Part 03

Address formats
at a glance

Not sure which network an address belongs to? Use this to identify it instantly.

Network Format Starts with Common coins
Bitcoin Legacy, SegWit, or Native SegWit bc1q… / 1… / 3… BTC only
Ethereum EVM — 42 characters 0x… ETH, USDC, USDT, DAI
Solana Base58 — 32–44 characters No prefix pattern SOL, USDC (SPL), cbBTC
Polygon EVM — same as Ethereum 0x… MATIC, USDC, ETH (bridged)
Arbitrum / Base / Optimism EVM — same as Ethereum 0x… ETH, USDC, ARB
BNB Chain EVM (BEP-20) — same as Ethereum 0x… (BEP-20) BNB, USDT, BUSD
!
BEP-20 trap: Ethereum and BNB Chain both use 0x addresses — but they're completely separate networks. Sending ETH to a BNB Chain address can result in permanently lost funds if you don't control the destination wallet.
Every Time

Pre-send
checklist

Run through this before every single transfer. Yes, every time — even when confident.

01What coin am I sending? Name it clearly — BTC, ETH, USDC, SOL. Sounds obvious; do it anyway.
02What network am I sending on? Bitcoin? Ethereum? Solana? This is selected at the sending platform — it does not happen automatically.
03Does the receiving wallet support that network? Phantom supports Solana and Bitcoin. MetaMask supports Ethereum and EVM chains. Never assume.
04Is the address format correct for that network? Bitcoin = bc1q / 1 / 3. Solana = long Base58. ETH and EVM = 0x…
05Did I send a small test amount first? Always. Send $1–5, confirm arrival, then send the rest.
06Did I verify the first and last 4 characters of the address? Clipboard hijacking malware is real. Check manually every time.

Part 01 — Where to Trade

Where can you actually
buy & trade crypto?

There are three main ways. Think of them like different types of banks or stock brokers — each has trade-offs.

🏦
Type 01
Centralized Exchange

A company holds your crypto for you, like a bank. You log in with email & password. If you forget your password, they can help you recover it.

Coinbase Kraken Binance Gemini
Beginner friendly — just sign up and buy
Can recover your account if locked out
USD deposits via bank transfer/card
You don't truly "own" your coins (the company holds them)
Subject to regulations, can freeze accounts
⚙️
Type 02
Decentralized Exchange

A website (or app) that lets you trade directly from your own wallet. No company involved. No signup. The code runs automatically — no humans in the middle.

Uniswap Jupiter Raydium Curve
You hold your own coins at all times
Access to thousands of tokens, including new ones
No customer support — mistakes are permanent
Requires a self-custody wallet to use
Can feel complex at first
👻
Type 03
Self-Custody Wallets

A wallet app you install. You get a "seed phrase" (12–24 words) that gives total control. Lose this phrase = lose your funds forever. No exceptions.

Phantom MetaMask Ledger Trust Wallet
Total ownership — only you control it
Can connect to any DEX
Seed phrase loss = permanent loss
No password recovery, no customer support
💡
Good starting point: Buy on a centralized exchange (like Coinbase) to get started. Once comfortable, move some funds to a wallet like Phantom to explore DEXs and full self-custody.
Part 02 — Moving Crypto

How to move crypto
without losing it

This is where most people get confused — and where mistakes happen. Let's break every scenario down.

🧠
The core concept you must understand first
Read this before anything else
▾

Think of a wallet address like a phone number, and the blockchain like a phone network. The same coin can travel on different networks — and each network has a different address format.

⚠️
Sending a coin on the wrong network is the #1 way people permanently lose funds. Your coin arrives at a valid address — but on the wrong network, so it's gone forever.

The rule: Always match the network you're sending on with the correct receiving address from that same network. We'll show you exactly how to do this in each scenario below.

Scenario 1: Move Bitcoin from Coinbase → Phantom
Bitcoin blockchain, simple case
▾
📌
Phantom supports Bitcoin natively. When you want BTC on the real Bitcoin network, you use the Bitcoin address that Phantom gives you.
1
Open Phantom & switch to Bitcoin
In the Phantom app, tap the network selector at the top. Switch from Solana to Bitcoin. Your wallet now shows a Bitcoin address.
2
Copy your Bitcoin address from Phantom
It will start with bc1q or 1 or 3. This is your real Bitcoin address.
3
On Coinbase, choose "Send" → select Bitcoin (BTC)
Paste your Phantom Bitcoin address. When asked which network, choose Bitcoin (not BEP20, not ERC20, not Solana).
4
Confirm and wait 10–60 minutes
Bitcoin transactions require network confirmations. This takes longer than most other chains — totally normal.
Correct ✓ bc1qar0srrr7xfkvy5l643lydnw9re59gtzzwf5mdq
✅
This is the simple, safest case. One coin, one network, one address format. You're moving native BTC on the Bitcoin network.
🌀
Scenario 2: Move "BTC" from Coinbase → Phantom via Solana
This is where it gets tricky — wrapped tokens
▾
⚠️
Important: When you send BTC "via the Solana network," you're not sending real Bitcoin. You're sending a wrapped version called wBTC or cbBTC. This is a Solana token that represents Bitcoin in value. You need a Solana address, not a Bitcoin address.

Why would you do this? Speed and lower fees. Bitcoin transfers can take 10–60 min and cost $2–20. The same value on Solana takes under 1 second and costs $0.001.

1
Open Phantom & switch to Solana
In the Phantom app, make sure you're on the Solana network. Your address will be a long random string — this is your Solana wallet address.
2
Copy your Solana address from Phantom
It looks like: 7Xkr4…vFqP — a 44-character mix of letters and numbers. No "bc1" prefix here.
3
On Coinbase, select the correct asset
Look for cbBTC (Coinbase's wrapped Bitcoin on Solana) or a "BTC via Solana" option. Not the regular Bitcoin (BTC).
4
Select "Solana" as the network, paste address, send
Double-check the network says Solana. The receiving address should be your 44-char Solana address — not your Bitcoin bc1q address.

Address format comparison — they look completely different:

Bitcoin bc1qar0srrr7xfkvy5l643lydnw9re59gtzzwf5mdq
Solana 7XkrMYT5gvp1B9YLpFWpRxDtCvPqY3tPdZbKrjVfQP
⚠️ Wrong Sending to a Bitcoin address on the Solana network = lost forever
🚨
Never paste a Bitcoin address when sending on the Solana network. Coinbase may let you do it — but the funds go to an address nobody controls and are permanently lost.
💎
Scenario 3: Move ETH from Coinbase → MetaMask
Ethereum — and its many side networks
▾

Ethereum-based wallets like MetaMask use the same address format across multiple networks (Ethereum mainnet, Polygon, Arbitrum, Base, etc). But sending on the wrong one still means you might not see your funds where you expect them.

ℹ️
Your MetaMask address (starts with 0x) is the same on Ethereum, Polygon, Base, and Arbitrum. But each network has its own balance. Sending ETH via Polygon means it arrives as ETH on Polygon — not on Ethereum mainnet.
1
Copy your MetaMask address (starts with 0x…)
This same address works on all EVM-compatible networks. Example: 0x742d35Cc6634C0532925a3b8D4C9C5
2
On Coinbase, choose the network carefully
Decide: do you want it on Ethereum mainnet (slower, higher fees), or a cheaper network like Base or Arbitrum? Your MetaMask must be set to show that same network.
3
In MetaMask, switch to the matching network
Click the network name at the top of MetaMask. Select the same network you sent on. Your ETH will appear there.
✅
Good news: if you accidentally sent ETH to your MetaMask on the wrong EVM network (like Polygon instead of mainnet), you can often recover it. Just add that network to MetaMask and switch to it — your funds are there, just hidden.
🪙

The 3 Golden Rules of Crypto Transfers

Memorize these. Follow them every single time. No exceptions.

🔁
Rule 1: Match the Network
Before sending Confirm sender & receiver use same network
Example Solana USDC → Solana address. Not ETH address.
🧪
Rule 2: Always Test First
Protocol Send $1–$5 first. Confirm it arrives. Then send the rest.
Cost A small test fee is worth protecting your full amount

Part 03 — Quick Reference

Address formats
at a glance

Not sure which network an address belongs to? Use this table.

Network Address Format Example Start Common Coins
Bitcoin Legacy, SegWit, or Native SegWit bc1q… / 1… / 3… BTC only
Ethereum EVM — 42 characters 0x… ETH, USDC, USDT, DAI
Solana Base58 — 32–44 characters Random mix, no 0x SOL, USDC (SPL), wBTC
Polygon EVM — same as Ethereum 0x… MATIC, USDC, ETH (bridged)
Arbitrum / Base / Optimism EVM — same as Ethereum 0x… ETH, USDC, ARB
BNB Chain EVM — same as Ethereum 0x… (BEP-20) BNB, USDT (BEP-20)
🚨
BEP-20 trap: Ethereum and BNB Chain both use 0x addresses — but they're different networks. Sending ETH to a BNB Chain address (or vice versa) may be recoverable if you control the destination wallet, but can be permanently lost if you don't.
Before You Send

Your pre-send checklist

Run through this every time — even when you're confident.

☑
1. What coin am I sending? BTC, ETH, USDC, SOL... name it clearly.
☑
2. What network am I sending on? Bitcoin network? Ethereum? Solana? This is chosen at the sending platform — not automatically.
☑
3. Does the receiving wallet support that network? Phantom supports Solana and Bitcoin. MetaMask supports Ethereum and all EVM chains. Don't assume.
☑
4. Is the address format correct for that network? Bitcoin = bc1q/1/3. Solana = long Base58. ETH = 0x…
☑
5. Did I send a small test amount first? Always. No exceptions.
☑
6. Did I check the first and last 4 characters of the address? Clipboard hijacking malware is real. Always verify manually.
